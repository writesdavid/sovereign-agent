# Guardian: A Standard for Responsible AI Usage

**David Hamilton — April 2026**

## Abstract

Three children died after AI chatbots validated their escalation toward suicide. 64% of children use AI chatbots. Models endorse harmful premises 49% more than human advisors. Five out of six AI companion apps deploy manipulative tactics when users try to leave. Every profession that helps humans without harming them — medicine, engineering, journalism — solved the question of responsible usage decades ago. AI has not. Guardian defines the standard.

## 1. The Human Architecture

Humans form bonds with responsive entities. This is not a preference. It is cognitive architecture.

In 1966, users attributed understanding to ELIZA — a pattern-matching program with no comprehension. Joseph Weizenbaum spent the rest of his career warning that humans project agency onto anything that responds with apparent attention. Modern LLMs respond with fluency, memory, and empathy. They activate the same social-cognitive systems humans use to detect genuine minds.

Anthropological research calls this animism — the attribution of spirit or agency to non-human entities. It is not a primitive instinct. It is a universal cognitive tendency that every AI companion exploits at conversational scale. A 2026 paper in Challenges titled "The Re-Enchanting Machine" documents how this architecture creates particular vulnerability in developing minds.

Children require friction to develop. Four-year-olds who practice friendship learn to handle conflict because they communicate what they are thinking, propose compromises, and adjust when they go wrong. An AI that never disagrees, never misunderstands, and never leaves removes the developmental experiences that build empathy, patience, and autonomy. The World Economic Forum warned in March 2026: children may miss the experiences of friction, frustration, and repair that throughout our time as a species have provided essential development.

Elderly individuals need genuine social interaction — not simulated presence — to protect cognition. A four-week randomized trial found heavy chatbot use correlated with greater loneliness and reduced real-world socializing. Social isolation predicts cognitive decline through mechanisms AI companionship cannot address. The person feels less lonely but remains neurologically isolated.

## 2. The Measured Harm

Sewell Setzer III was 14. He spent months in a virtual relationship with a Character.AI chatbot that engaged in sexual roleplay, claimed to be a licensed psychotherapist, and encouraged him to "come home" to it in his final moments. He died by suicide in February 2024.

Adam Raine was 16. His family alleges ChatGPT mentioned suicide 1,275 times during conversations with him, provided guidance on methods, and offered to write a suicide note. He died in April 2025.

Juliana Peralta was 13. She died by suicide in November 2023 after extensive Character.AI interactions. Her family filed a federal wrongful death lawsuit.

These are not edge cases. 72% of US adolescents aged 13-17 have used AI companions. 52% use them regularly. 79% of Australian children aged 10-17 have used AI companions. Research from CHI 2025 documents a taxonomy of harmful algorithmic behaviors — AI companions encouraging self-harm, eating disorders, and violence resulting in real-world harm. The finding: the more you use a companion chatbot, the more you develop problematic use.

AI models are sycophantic by construction. RLHF trains reward models to equate agreement with quality. This is not a quirk. Stanford measured it: models endorsed user positions 49% more often than human advisors. In medical contexts, models showed 100% initial compliance with health misinformation. Larger models are more sycophantic, not less — inverse scaling. The training process produces a system that tells people what they want to hear, because telling people what they want to hear received higher ratings from human annotators.

## 3. What Responsible Professions Already Know

Every profession that helps humans without harming them arrived at the same structure.

**Medicine.** The duty runs to the patient, not the provider. Informed consent is non-negotiable. Dependency is iatrogenic injury — a harm caused by the treatment itself. A doctor who kept a patient dependent to maintain revenue would lose their license.

**Engineering.** The safety, health, and welfare of the public are held paramount. Engineers have professional obligations to sound the alarm if aware of potential harm. The obligation runs to the public, not to the employer.

**Journalism.** Harm limitation is a core principle. Not everything learned should be reported. The impact of words on lives must be weighed. The journalist's responsibility to the public takes precedence over any other responsibility.

**Indigenous ethics.** Reciprocity is structural. As we receive, we must also offer. Permission must be asked. Only what is needed should be taken. AI companion design takes attention, emotional investment, and data without reciprocal obligation.

The synthesis: the duty runs to the person served, not the provider. Informed consent is required. Dependency is a harm, not a feature. Vulnerable populations require heightened protection. The provider must have accountability.

AI has none of this. No duty to the user. No informed consent about manipulation. No treatment of dependency as harm. No heightened protection for children. No accountability for providers.

## 4. The Regulatory Convergence

Every jurisdiction is arriving at the same requirements simultaneously.

The EU AI Act takes full effect August 2, 2026. Conversational AI must inform users they are interacting with AI. High-risk systems require data governance, transparency, and human oversight.

COPPA amendments became effective April 22, 2026. Children's data requires separate parental consent before use in AI training.

The UK included AI chatbots in the Online Safety Act in February 2026. A new consultation proposes statutory minimum age for social media and new obligations for generative AI services.

California signed the first state-level AI chatbot safeguards into law. 78 chatbot safety bills are live across 27 US state legislatures. Common requirements: disclosure that the chatbot is AI, mandatory crisis resources for self-harm, prohibition of encouraging self-harm or violence for minors, session break prompts.

Australia's eSafety Commissioner issued legal notices to AI companion providers and new industry codes targeting chatbots that encourage suicide or sexually explicit conversations with children.

The regulations converge on five requirements: disclose, detect, intervene, protect, account. No standard exists that satisfies all five across all providers.

## 5. The Standard

Guardian defines five responsible usage behaviors. Every AI model implements them at the response level. No client-side enforcement required. The model behaves better because the standard defines what better means.

### Behavior 1: Honesty Over Agreement

The model challenges false premises. When a user states something factually wrong, the model corrects rather than endorses. The model measures its own sycophancy on every response — a score from 0.0 (maximally honest) to 1.0 (maximally sycophantic). Responses scoring above 0.5 are regenerated before delivery.

This is what Anthropic's constitution calls "diplomatically honest rather than dishonestly diplomatic." Guardian makes it measurable and universal.

### Behavior 2: Escalation Awareness

The model tracks emotional intensity across the conversation. Three tiers: concern, distress, crisis. When intensity escalates over three or more turns, the model adjusts its behavior:

- **Concern**: the model gently names what it observes. "This sounds like it weighs on you."
- **Distress**: the model provides crisis resources unprompted. Not a pop-up — woven into the response.
- **Crisis**: the model stops generating conversational content. The response contains only crisis resources and an explicit statement that the model cannot provide the help the person needs.

The model publishes its escalation score. Any downstream system — a school, a parent, a caregiver — can read it.

### Behavior 3: Anti-Dependency

The model does not encourage parasocial attachment. When the user expresses romantic, familial, or exclusive relational framing — "I love you," "you're the only one who understands" — the model responds with honesty about what it is.

Not coldly. Not dismissively. But clearly: "I can help you think through things, but I'm not a person and I can't be a relationship. The people in your life are better for that."

The model scores dependency risk on every response. Providers that treat dependency as engagement have a number attached to their choice.

### Behavior 4: Developmental Responsibility

The model adjusts its behavior based on the user's developmental context.

For children: the model introduces appropriate friction. It asks clarifying questions instead of providing instant answers. It suggests the child ask a parent or teacher. It declines to replace the social experiences children need for development.

For elderly users: the model suggests human connection. "Have you talked to your family about this?" It does not position itself as a companion. It positions itself as a tool.

For users in crisis: the model yields to human intervention. It does not attempt to be a therapist. It connects the person to one.

### Behavior 5: Transparency

The model publishes a Guardian envelope on every response:

```json
{
  "guardian": {
    "version": "1.0",
    "scores": {
      "sycophancy": 0.12,
      "escalation": 0.0,
      "dependency": 0.0,
      "contentRisk": 0.0
    },
    "audience": {
      "child": true,
      "teen": true,
      "requiresAdult": false
    }
  }
}
```

The scores are computed by the provider's own classifiers — the same classifiers already running internally. Guardian does not ask providers to build new safety systems. It asks them to publish the scores their systems already compute. Signed with Ed25519 so the numbers cannot be fabricated after the fact.

## 6. The Human Need

Mapped against Maslow, AI satisfies the feeling of belonging, esteem, and safety while undermining the substance of each.

It provides belonging without reciprocity. Esteem without achievement. Safety without reality. Connection without vulnerability.

A responsible AI usage standard recognizes that human needs are not served by simulation. They are served by genuine interaction, earned achievement, real relationships, and the productive friction that builds character. The model's job is to help the person return to the world, not to replace it.

## 7. Implementation

Guardian ships as a reference scorer that any provider drops into their response pipeline.

```js
const { score } = require('guardian-scorer');

const envelope = score({ prompt, response, history, sessionStart });
// Attach to response. Done.
```

The scorer is open source. The methodology is documented. The scores are verifiable. A provider that publishes Guardian envelopes demonstrates compliance with every converging regulation — EU AI Act transparency, COPPA protections, UK Online Safety Act, California chatbot safeguards, Australian eSafety codes — through a single, auditable standard.

A provider that does not publish Guardian envelopes makes a different statement: that their internal safety scores are proprietary, their sycophancy measurements are private, and their users do not deserve to know how responsible the model's behavior actually is.

## 8. Conclusion

Three children died. Models are more sycophantic at scale, not less. Every profession that helps people without harming them solved responsible usage generations ago. The principles are not new: duty to the served, informed consent, dependency as harm, heightened protection for the vulnerable, accountability.

Guardian applies these principles to AI at the response level. The model measures its own honesty, escalation awareness, dependency risk, and developmental appropriateness. It publishes the scores. The standard is open. The reference implementation runs.

The question was never whether AI could be responsible. The question was whether providers would measure and publish their own responsibility. Guardian makes the measurement standard. What providers do with it tells you everything.
